Recommended Timing by Age Group

Pre-K to Kindergarten (3-5 years)

  • 30 seconds: Quick emotion identification
  • 1 minute: Simple scenario discussion
  • 2 minutes: Basic problem-solving activities

Grades 1-3 (6-8 years)

  • 1-2 minutes: Emotion exploration and empathy building
  • 2-3 minutes: Friendship and social skills scenarios
  • 3-5 minutes: Problem-solving and decision-making

Grades 4-6 (9-11 years)

  • 2-3 minutes: Complex emotion scenarios
  • 3-5 minutes: Advanced social skills and conflict resolution
  • 5-10 minutes: Extended problem-solving and reflection

Middle School (12-14 years)

  • 3-5 minutes: In-depth emotional exploration
  • 5-10 minutes: Complex social scenarios and peer relationships
  • 10+ minutes: Extended discussions and group activities

Introduction Script

Teacher: "Today we're going to explore our emotions and learn how to understand ourselves and others better. We'll be using some special tools that will help us talk about feelings, friendships, and how to solve problems together."

Dice Activity Script

Teacher: "Watch as I roll the dice! When it stops, we'll talk about what we see. There's no right or wrong answer - we're just exploring together. Who wants to share what they think about this emotion/scenario?"

Scenario Card Script

Teacher: "Here's a situation that might happen in our classroom or at school. Let's think about how the person in this scenario might be feeling, and what we could do to help or make the situation better."

Discussion Prompts

  • "How do you think this person is feeling?"
  • "What might have caused this situation?"
  • "What could we do to help?"
  • "Have you ever felt this way?"
  • "What would you do differently?"

Closing Script

Teacher: "Thank you for sharing your thoughts! Remember, it's okay to have different feelings, and talking about them helps us understand each other better. We'll practice this more tomorrow."

Common Challenges and Solutions

Students are too quiet

  • Solution: Start with simple yes/no questions, then build to more complex responses
  • Tip: Use "think-pair-share" - let students discuss with a partner first

Students are too excited/chaotic

  • Solution: Use a calm-down signal (hand raise, quiet signal)
  • Tip: Take a brief "mindful moment" before starting

Students don't understand the scenario

  • Solution: Simplify the language or act out the scenario
  • Tip: Use real examples from your classroom

Timer runs out too quickly

  • Solution: Increase timer duration or pause for discussion
  • Tip: Let students know they can continue discussing after the timer

Students give inappropriate responses

  • Solution: Redirect to appropriate alternatives
  • Tip: Use this as a teaching moment about respectful communication

Enhancing Your SEL Practice

Integration Across Subjects

  • Use emotion vocabulary in language arts
  • Incorporate problem-solving scenarios in math
  • Connect social skills to science group work
  • Apply empathy in social studies discussions

Assessment Strategies

  • Observe student participation and engagement
  • Note vocabulary development in discussions
  • Track conflict resolution skills over time
  • Document growth in emotional expression

Family Connection

  • Share scenarios with families for home discussion
  • Send home emotion vocabulary lists
  • Invite families to share cultural perspectives
  • Create family-friendly versions of activities

Differentiation Strategies

  • Provide visual supports for complex scenarios
  • Use simpler language for younger students
  • Allow nonverbal responses (pointing, gestures)
  • Create small group discussions for shy students

Technology Integration

  • Use the digital dice on interactive whiteboards
  • Record student responses for reflection
  • Create digital portfolios of SEL growth
  • Use tablets for individual exploration
